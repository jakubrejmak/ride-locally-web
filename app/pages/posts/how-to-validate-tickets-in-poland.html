# How to Validate Your Ticket on Polish Public Transport So you've bought a paper ticket and you're standing inside a tram or bus, holding a small rectangle of paper, wondering — *now what?* Welcome to the art of **kasowanie**, a rite of passage for anyone riding Polish public transport. ## What Does "Validate" Even Mean? In Poland, buying a ticket isn't enough. Your ticket is only valid once it's been **punched (skasowany)** — stamped with the current date and time by a small machine mounted inside the vehicle. An unvalidated ticket is the same as no ticket at all, and inspectors won't care that you paid for it. ## Step by Step Find the **kasownik** — a small yellow, orange, or grey box mounted on poles or walls inside the vehicle. There are usually 2–4 per bus or tram, often near the doors. Insert your ticket into the slot, printed side facing you. You'll hear a click or a beep, and the machine stamps the date and time onto the ticket. Pull it out and check — if there's a fresh print on it, you're good. That's it. Keep the ticket on you for the entire ride. You may need to show it to an inspector (**kontroler**) at any point. ## Common Mistakes **Validating too late.** You should validate immediately after boarding. Doing it when you see an inspector walking toward you is a classic move — and they've seen it a thousand times. **Wrong side up.** If the stamp is missing or illegible, try again or use another machine. An unreadable stamp can still get you fined. **Assuming you don't need to validate.** Some tickets bought via mobile apps or contactless payment are pre-validated — but traditional paper tickets are never valid until punched. When in doubt, punch it. **Using an expired ticket type.** Time-limited tickets (e.g. 20-minute, 75-minute) start counting from the moment of validation, not from purchase. Make sure you pick the right duration for your journey. ## What About Mobile Tickets? If you bought your ticket through a mobile app (like Jakdojade, mKomunikacja, or your city's official app), it's **already validated** — the QR code and timestamp serve as proof. Just have your phone ready to show the screen if an inspector asks. ## What Happens if You Don't Validate? Ticket inspectors (**kontrolerzy**) can board at any stop and ask to see your ticket. If it's not validated, you'll face an on-the-spot fine — typically **280 PLN** as of 2025, though this can vary by city. You can sometimes pay a reduced fine if you settle immediately, but it's still not cheap. Inspectors in Poland wear visible ID badges and are required to identify themselves. They cannot physically detain you, but refusing to provide your details or pay can escalate the situation — including involvement of the city guard (**straż miejska**) or police. ## Quick Tips The kasownik sometimes jams or runs out of ink — if none of the machines work, note the time and vehicle number in case of inspection. Some cities accept this as a valid excuse, others don't, so validate at the first opportunity if you transfer to another vehicle. On newer trams and buses in major cities, you might also see contactless card readers near the doors. Tapping your bank card works like a mobile ticket — it charges the fare and counts as validated automatically. Peak hours mean crowded vehicles. If you can't reach a kasownik right away, don't panic — just get to one as soon as the crowd thins. Inspectors generally understand this, but "I couldn't reach it" after 5 stops is a harder sell. ## Glossary - **Kasownik** — the ticket validator machine - **Kasować / skasować** — to validate / punch a ticket - **Bilet jednorazowy** — single-ride ticket - **Bilet czasowy** — time-limited ticket (e.g. 20 min, 75 min, 24h) - **Kontroler / kontrolerka** — ticket inspector - **Mandat** — fine
